📌 Introduction – Why Grammar Rules Matter

English grammar rules are the building blocks of the English language. They help us communicate clearly and correctly. Whether you're preparing for BCS, IELTS, Bank Jobs, or University Admission, understanding these 100 essential rules will give you a strong foundation.

📖 How to Use This Guide:
  • Read each rule carefully – Understand the concept
  • Study the example – See how the rule is applied
  • Click the link – Go to the detailed article for more explanation
  • Practice – Use the tools and quizzes

Let's start with the most fundamental topic — Parts of Speech.

📚 Rules 1-20: Parts of Speech

1
Every sentence must have a verb.
✅ She is a teacher. • He goes to school. • They eat rice.
📖 Learn More →
2
A noun is the name of a person, place, thing, or idea.
✅ Rahim, Dhaka, book, honesty
📖 Learn More →
3
A pronoun replaces a noun to avoid repetition.
✅ Rahim is a student. He studies hard. (Instead of Rahim again)
📖 Learn More →
4
An adjective describes a noun or pronoun.
✅ She is a beautiful girl. • He is tall.
📖 Learn More →
5
An adverb describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb.
✅ She sings beautifully. • He runs fast.
📖 Learn More →
6
A preposition shows the relationship between a noun/pronoun and another word.
✅ The book is on the table. • She went to school.
📖 Learn More →
7
A conjunction joins words, phrases, or clauses.
✅ I like tea and coffee. • He is poor but honest.
📖 Learn More →
8
An interjection expresses sudden emotion.
✅ Wow! That's amazing. • Oh! I forgot my keys.
📖 Learn More →
9
Proper nouns always start with a capital letter.
✅ Rahim, Dhaka, Bangladesh (not rahim, dhaka, bangladesh)
📖 Learn More →
10
Common nouns are general names — not specific.
✅ boy, city, river (not specific like Rahim, Dhaka)
📖 Learn More →
11
Abstract nouns name ideas, qualities, or feelings — not physical objects.
✅ honesty, freedom, love, success
📖 Learn More →
12
Collective nouns can be singular or plural depending on meaning.
✅ The team is winning. (as one unit) • The team are arguing. (as individuals)
📖 Learn More →
13
Transitive verbs require an object.
✅ She reads a book. (Object: a book)
📖 Learn More →
14
Intransitive verbs do not require an object.
✅ She sleeps. • He cried. (No object needed)
📖 Learn More →
15
Linking verbs connect the subject to a complement (adjective/noun).
✅ She is beautiful. • He became a doctor. • It smells good.
📖 Learn More →
16
Action verbs show physical or mental action.
✅ He runs. • She thinks. • They eat.
📖 Learn More →
17
Stative verbs describe a state, not an action — they don't take continuous form.
✅ I know the answer. (NOT: I am knowing) • I agree. (NOT: I am agreeing)
📖 Learn More →
18
Demonstrative adjectives show which one: this, that, these, those.
✅ This book is mine. • Those chairs are new.
📖 Learn More →
19
Possessive adjectives show ownership: my, your, his, her, its, our, their.
✅ This is my book. • Is this your pen?
📖 Learn More →
20
Interrogative adjectives ask questions: which, what, whose.
✅ Which book do you want? • Whose bag is this?
📖 Learn More →

📝 Rules 21-35: Sentence Structure

21
Every sentence must have a subject and a verb.
✅ He goes to school. (Subject: He, Verb: goes)
22
A simple sentence has one independent clause.
✅ She reads a book. • He runs fast.
23
A compound sentence has two independent clauses joined by a conjunction.
✅ He worked hard, so he passed. • I like tea, but she likes coffee.
📖 Learn More →
24
A complex sentence has one independent clause and one dependent clause.
✅ Because it was raining, I stayed home. • I stayed home because it was raining.
📖 Learn More →
25
A compound-complex sentence has two independent clauses and one dependent clause.
✅ Although it was raining, I went out, and I bought a book.
26
The object receives the action of the verb.
✅ He reads a book. (Object: a book)
27
The complement completes the meaning of the subject.
✅ She is beautiful. (Complement: beautiful)
28
Adjectives usually come before the noun they describe.
✅ Beautiful girl, tall tree, red car
📖 Learn More →
29
Adverbs can come before or after the verb.
✅ She slowly walked. • She walked slowly.
📖 Learn More →
30
Adverbs of frequency (always, never, often) come before the main verb.
✅ She always comes late. • I never lie.
📖 Learn More →
31
Adverbs of manner (quickly, slowly) come after the verb.
✅ She sings beautifully. • He runs fast.
📖 Learn More →
32
A prepositional phrase starts with a preposition and ends with a noun/pronoun.
✅ On the table • In the room • At school
📖 Learn More →
33
A sentence should not end with a preposition in formal writing.
✅ This is the book for which I was looking. (Formal) • This is the book I was looking for. (Informal)
34
A subject can be a noun, pronoun, or noun phrase.
✅ The boy plays cricket. • She sings well. • Reading books is fun.
35
A sentence must express a complete thought.
✅ She went to the market and bought some vegetables. (Complete) ❌ Went to the market. (Incomplete)

📝 Rules 36-50: Subject-Verb Agreement

36
Singular subject → Singular verb (verb + s/es).
✅ He goes to school. • She writes a letter.
📖 Learn More →
37
Plural subject → Plural verb (no s/es).
✅ They go to school. • The boys write letters.
📖 Learn More →
38
Everyone, someone, anyone, nobody — are singular → take singular verb.
✅ Everyone is present. • Someone has taken my pen.
📖 Learn More →
39
One of + Plural Noun → Singular verb.
✅ One of the students is absent.
📖 Learn More →
40
A number of → Plural verb. The number of → Singular verb.
✅ A number of students are present. • The number of students is fifty.
📖 Learn More →
41
Neither...nor / Either...or → verb agrees with the closest subject.
✅ Neither the teacher nor the students are present. • Neither the students nor the teacher is present.
📖 Learn More →
42
Collective nouns → Singular verb (as one unit).
✅ The team is winning. • The family is going on vacation.
📖 Learn More →
43
News, mathematics, physics, politics → Singular verb.
✅ The news is good. • Mathematics is difficult.
📖 Learn More →
44
Each, every, either, neither → Singular verb.
✅ Each has a book. • Every is welcome. • Either is fine.
📖 Learn More →
45
Amounts, measurements, time → Singular verb (as one unit).
✅ Ten dollars is a lot of money. • Three years is a long time.
📖 Learn More →
46
Two subjects joined by "and" → Plural verb.
✅ Rahim and Karim are friends.
📖 Learn More →
47
Two subjects joined by "and" (same person) → Singular verb.
✅ The director and producer is coming. (One person with two titles)
📖 Learn More →
48
Relative pronouns (who, which, that) → verb agrees with the antecedent.
✅ The boy who is sitting there is my brother. (boy singular → is) • The boys who are sitting there are my brothers. (boys plural → are)
📖 Learn More →
49
None can be singular or plural (both accepted).
✅ None of the students is present. (Formal) • None of the students are present. (Modern)
📖 Learn More →
50
There is/are → verb agrees with the subject after it.
✅ There is a book on the table. • There are many books on the table.
📖 Learn More →

⏳ Rules 51-65: Tenses

51
Present Indefinite: Subject + V1 (s/es for third person singular) — Habitual actions, universal truths.
✅ He goes to school. • The sun rises in the east.
📖 Learn More →
52
Present Continuous: Subject + am/is/are + Ving — Action happening now.
✅ He is reading a book. • They are playing cricket.
📖 Learn More →
53
Present Perfect: Subject + have/has + V3 — Past action with present result.
✅ I have read the book. • She has gone to school.
📖 Learn More →
54
Present Perfect Continuous: Subject + have/has + been + Ving — Started in the past and continues.
✅ I have been studying for three hours.
📖 Learn More →
55
Past Indefinite: Subject + V2 — Completed action in the past.
✅ I went to school yesterday. • She ate rice.
📖 Learn More →
56
Past Continuous: Subject + was/were + Ving — Action in progress in the past.
✅ He was reading when I arrived.
📖 Learn More →
57
Past Perfect: Subject + had + V3 — Action completed before another past action.
✅ I had finished the work before he came.
📖 Learn More →
58
Past Perfect Continuous: Subject + had + been + Ving — Ongoing action up to a point in the past.
✅ I had been studying for two hours when he arrived.
📖 Learn More →
59
Future Indefinite: Subject + will + V1 — Action in the future.
✅ I will go to school tomorrow.
📖 Learn More →
60
Future Continuous: Subject + will + be + Ving — Ongoing action at a specific future time.
✅ I will be reading at 8 PM tomorrow.
📖 Learn More →
61
Future Perfect: Subject + will + have + V3 — Completed by a specific future time.
✅ I will have finished the work by tomorrow.
📖 Learn More →
62
Future Perfect Continuous: Subject + will + have + been + Ving — Ongoing action up to a future point.
✅ I will have been studying for two hours by the time you arrive.
📖 Learn More →
63
Modal verbs (can, could, may, might, must, should, will, would) + V1 (base form).
✅ I can go. • She must study. • He will come.
📖 Learn More →
64
Stative verbs (know, believe, belong, seem) — never take continuous form.
✅ I know the answer. (NOT: I am knowing) • She believes in honesty. (NOT: She is believing)
📖 Learn More →
65
Since + point in time, For + duration.
✅ I have been here since 2015. • I have been here for three hours.
📖 Learn More →

📋 Rules 66-75: Articles

66
Use "a" before words that start with a consonant sound.
✅ a book, a cat, a university (U sounds like 'Y')
📖 Learn More →
67
Use "an" before words that start with a vowel sound.
✅ an apple, an hour (H silent), an MBA (M sounds like 'Em')
📖 Learn More →
68
Use "the" for unique objects.
✅ The sun, the moon, the earth
📖 Learn More →
69
Use "the" before superlatives.
✅ She is the best student. • He is the tallest boy.
📖 Learn More →
70
Use "the" before musical instruments.
✅ She plays the piano. • He plays the guitar.
📖 Learn More →
71
Use "the" before ordinal numbers.
✅ He was the first to arrive. • She stood the third.
📖 Learn More →
72
Use "the" before rivers, seas, oceans, and mountain ranges.
✅ The Padma, The Bay of Bengal, The Himalayas
📖 Learn More →
73
No article before languages, meals, sports, and proper nouns.
✅ I speak English. (NOT: The English) • I had breakfast. (NOT: The breakfast) • He plays cricket. (NOT: The cricket)
📖 Learn More →
74
No article before single mountains.
✅ Mount Everest (NOT: The Mount Everest) • Mount Fuji (NOT: The Mount Fuji)
📖 Learn More →
75
"The" + adjective = group of people.
✅ The rich should help the poor. • The blind need special care.
📖 Learn More →

📍 Rules 76-85: Prepositions

76
At + specific time, In + months/years, On + days/dates.
✅ at 5 PM, in July, on Monday
📖 Learn More →
77
Afraid of (not afraid from).
✅ She is afraid of spiders. (NOT: afraid from)
📖 Learn More →
78
Good at (not good in).
✅ He is good at English. (NOT: good in)
📖 Learn More →
79
Interested in (not interested for).
✅ She is interested in learning English. (NOT: interested for)
📖 Learn More →
80
Senior to (not senior than).
✅ He is senior to me. (NOT: senior than)
📖 Learn More →
81
Married to (not married with).
✅ She is married to a doctor. (NOT: married with)
📖 Learn More →
82
Different from (not different than).
✅ This book is different from mine. (NOT: different than)
📖 Learn More →
83
Responsible for, Famous for, Proud of.
✅ He is responsible for the project. • Bangladesh is famous for its beauty. • She is proud of her achievements.
📖 Learn More →
84
Between = two items; Among = three or more items.
✅ Between you and me. • Among the students.
📖 Learn More →
85
Beside = next to; Besides = in addition to.
✅ Sit beside me. • I have other books besides this one.

🔄 Rules 86-90: Pronouns

86
Subject pronouns: I, You, He, She, It, We, They.
✅ He is a teacher. • She sings well.
📖 Learn More →
87
Object pronouns: Me, You, Him, Her, It, Us, Them.
✅ She gave the book to me. • I saw him yesterday.
📖 Learn More →
88
Use "I" as subject, "me" as object.
✅ I am a student. (Subject) • She gave the book to me. (Object)
📖 Learn More →
89
Who = subject; Whom = object.
✅ Who is coming? (Subject) • Whom did you see? (Object)
📖 Learn More →
90
Reflexive pronouns: myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves.
✅ He hurt himself. • She taught herself English.
📖 Learn More →

🎨 Rules 91-95: Adjectives & Adverbs

91
Adjectives describe nouns; adverbs describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs.
✅ She is a beautiful girl. (Adjective) • She sings beautifully. (Adverb)
📖 Learn More →
92
Hard = with effort; Hardly = almost not.
✅ He works hard. • He hardly works.
📖 Learn More →
93
Late = after expected time; Lately = recently.
✅ He arrived late. • I have been busy lately.
📖 Learn More →
94
Fewer = countable nouns; Less = uncountable nouns.
✅ Fewer books, Less water
📖 Learn More →
95
Elder = family relationship; Older = general comparison.
✅ He is my elder brother. • He is older than me.
📖 Learn More →

🔗 Rules 96-100: Conjunctions & Others

96
Coordinating conjunctions (FANBOYS): For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So.
✅ I like tea and coffee. • He is poor but honest.
📖 Learn More →
97
Subordinating conjunctions: because, although, since, while, if, unless, when, after, before.
✅ I stayed home because it was raining. • Although he is rich, he is humble.
📖 Learn More →
98
Correlative conjunctions: either...or, neither...nor, both...and, not only...but also.
✅ Either Rahim or Karim will come. • Neither Rahim nor Karim came.
📖 Learn More →
99
Don't use "because" and "so" together. Don't use "although" and "but" together.
❌ Because it was raining, so I stayed home. ✅ Because it was raining, I stayed home. ❌ Although he is rich, but he is humble. ✅ Although he is rich, he is humble.
📖 Learn More →
100
A sentence must start with a capital letter and end with a punctuation mark.
✅ He is a student. • She sings well. • What is your name?

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What are the most important English grammar rules?
The most important rules include: Subject-Verb Agreement (Rule 1-15), Tense Rules (Rule 20-35), Article Rules (Rule 45-55), Preposition Rules (Rule 60-70), and Sentence Structure Rules (Rule 80-90). These form the foundation of English grammar.
How many grammar rules are there in English?
There are hundreds of grammar rules in English, but this guide covers the 100 most essential rules that every learner should know. These rules cover Parts of Speech, Sentence Structure, Tenses, Subject-Verb Agreement, Articles, Prepositions, and more.
What is the first grammar rule to learn?
The first rule is Rule 1: Every sentence must have a verb. Without a verb, a sentence is incomplete. This is the most fundamental rule of English grammar.
What is the most common grammar rule tested in BCS?
Subject-Verb Agreement (Rules 36-50) is the most frequently tested topic in BCS and Bank Job exams. Also, Tense Rules (51-65), Articles (66-75), and Prepositions (76-85) are very common.
What is the difference between 'a' and 'an'?
Use 'a' before words that start with a consonant sound (a book, a university). Use 'an' before words that start with a vowel sound (an apple, an hour). The rule depends on sound, not spelling.
What is Subject-Verb Agreement?
Subject-Verb Agreement means that the verb must match the subject in number (singular or plural) and person. If the subject is singular, the verb must be singular; if plural, the verb must be plural.
What is the rule for 'everyone' and 'someone'?
'Everyone', 'someone', 'anyone', 'nobody', and 'each' are singular indefinite pronouns. They always take a singular verb. For example: 'Everyone is present' (not 'are').
What is the difference between 'since' and 'for'?
'Since' is used with a specific point in time (since 2015, since Monday). 'For' is used with a duration of time (for three hours, for five years).
What is the rule for 'Neither...nor' and 'Either...or'?
With 'Neither...nor' and 'Either...or', the verb agrees with the subject that is closest to the verb. For example: 'Neither the teacher nor the students are present' (students is plural, so 'are').
What is the difference between 'few' and 'a few'?
'Few' means 'almost none' and has a negative meaning (Few people came = almost nobody came). 'A few' means 'some' and has a positive meaning (A few people came = some people came).
What is the difference between 'little' and 'a little'?
'Little' means 'almost nothing' and has a negative meaning (There is little water = almost no water). 'A little' means 'some' and has a positive meaning (There is a little water = some water is available).
What is the rule for 'who' and 'whom'?
'Who' is used as a subject pronoun (like 'he'), and 'whom' is used as an object pronoun (like 'him'). Use 'who' when the pronoun is the subject of the verb, and 'whom' when it is the object of the verb or preposition.
What is the rule for 'I' and 'me'?
Use 'I' as the subject of a verb (I am a student). Use 'me' as the object of a verb or preposition (She gave the book to me).

The best way is to practice regularly. Read each rule, understand the example, and then create your own sentences. Use the Ovidhan app's grammar tools and quizzes for daily practice.
